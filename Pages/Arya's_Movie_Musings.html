Arya's Movie Musings

✦

Arya's Movie Musings is a personal movie review platform designed to bring together thoughtful analysis, accessible recommendations, and cinematic visual identity.

Overview

As a lifelong movie enthusiast, I wanted a space where I could write, reflect, and share my thoughts on films.

The goal was to design a platform that feels authentically me, supports my writing workflow, and presents my reviews in a way that is clean, respectable, and visually expressive.

Research

Context Study

I love writing and reviewing movies that I have watched, but existing platforms didn't give me a space that felt personal, expressive, or creatively inspiring. Social-driven platforms like Letterboxd emphasize feeds, likes, and public visibility while data-driven sites focus on metadata rather than personal reflection.

I needed a platform where I could:

  • Write freely without pressure
  • Organize my reviews in a clean, readable way
  • Express my personality through visual design
  • Maintain a simple workflow that encourages consistent writing
Arya's Movie Musings was designed and created to be a personal, distraction-free movie review website that reflects my voice and my writing habits.

Target Audience

I identified four users:

  • Primary users:
    • Myself
    • I designed the site to support my own writing habits
  • Secondary users
    • Readers who enjoy my writing or are looking for another review site, or enjoy reading movie reviews such as:
      • Cinephiles and film students
      • Casual viewers
      • Genre Fans (Horror, Sci-Fi, Action, etc.)
    Even though this is a personal site, it needs to be usable and enjoyable for others.

Pain Points

Through self-reflection and light research into how people journal about movies, I identified several key pain points that shaped my design:

  • Existing platforms feel impersonal
    • Most movie sites prioritize ratings, algorithms, and social engagement. I wanted a space that feels like my own creative journal, not a full-on public forum.
  • Readers struggle with dense or poorly structured layouts
    • Long reviews can feel heavy if typography and spacing aren't optimized well. My writing should be easy and enjoyable to read.
  • No platform reflects my own personal aesthetic
    • Many movie sites have a generic visual identity or feel stuffy or academic. I want a site that expresses my own personality and makes the writing experience feel inspiring.
  • Mobile experiances are often poorly optimized
    • Many movie websites that I read myself are poorly adapted to mobile or tablet when I'm on said mobile or tablet. I need a site that can easily be read and understood from either mobile, tablet, or computer.

Competitive Audit

I did a competitive audit on two websites to inform me how people structure movie review websites from personal blogs to professional websites, and take away their ideas:

Moria - The Science Fiction, Horror, & Fantasy Film Review

Strengths

  • Simple color pallette

Weaknesses

  • Lacks color and polish
  • Feels cramped
  • Needs more categories and imagery

Key Takeaways

Moria highlighted how clear navigation and straightforward structure can make niche film content feel approachable, even when the visual design is minimal.

RogerEbert.com

Strengths

  • Good brand consistency

  • Clean layout

Weaknesses

Key Takeaways

RogerEbert.com demonstrated how consistent branding and robust search tools can build trust and help users quickly find the content they want.

Project Goals

The website needed to:

  • Have a clean reading experiance
    • Because the writing should be the focus
  • Have a playful, personal visual identity
    • Because this is my creative space
  • Have a simple navigation
    • Because readers should find reviews easily
  • A layout that supports both long and short reviews
    • Because my writing varies from what movie I watch, which shapes how I write each review
  • A layout that works not only on computer, but also on tablet and mobile
    • Because I read movie reviews on my phone and a tablet, not just on the computer

Use cases

I want to write and publish a new review quickly

I want readers to browse my reviews easily

I want the site to feel personal and expressive

I want the layout to support both short reactions and longer, deeper analysis

Ideation

Information Architecture

Based on the insights earlier, I created a sitemap to define the structure and hierarchy of the website. They have multiple pages for my interests, yet they are easy to navigate and clear to reduce overload and keep an uncluttered site for myself and potential readers.

Visual Design Direction

I created several stylesheets to establish the color palette, typography, imagery, and several of the UI elements. The goal was to create a warm, cinematic aesthetic that feels professional yet personal. The visual design incorporates soft colors to create a cozy, journal-like feel, playful typography that reflects my voice as a writer, a simple layout to keep the focus on the content and reduce overload, and consistent spacing to improve readability and flow. Overall, it supports both the emotional tone of my writing and the functional needs of readers.

Layout Exploration and Directions

I explored multiple layout concepts such as a card‑based layout that blended personality with structure, a list‑based layout that was streamlined for quick scanning and consecutive reading, a grid-based layout that showcased reviews like a gallery, and a minimalist blog‑style layout that focused entirely on long-form reading. Each layout concept had its own strengths and weaknesses, and as such, the challenge was to choose a layout that felt expressive enough to represent my voice while remaining easy to navigate, intuitive, and readable to visitors. In the end, I chose the card-based layout.

Why I went with a card-based layout:

  • Cards feel personal and expressive
    • They feel like flipping through a journal, which aligns with the visual design of the site- intimate, reflective, cozy, and creative.
  • Cards create a natural visual rhythm
    • Each review becomes its own “moment,” giving the homepage a sense of pacing and personality, which prevents the site from feeling like a dense wall of text.
  • Cards help readers browse intuitively
    • Users can scan titles, posters, and short excerpts quickly. This supports browsing and encourages exploration.
  • Cards support both short and long reviews
    • A few of my reviews are short; others are deep analyses. Cards adapt well to both, offering flexibility without breaking the layout.
The final layout blends card-based previews for personality and scannability, a single-column reading view for comfort and focus, and intuitive, clean navigation to keep the experience calm and uncluttered. This direction supports my needs as a writer and the needs of readers who want a clean, enjoyable way to explore my reviews.

Design

Low-fidelity Wireframes

I created a few low-fidelity wireframes in Figma to visualize consistent layouts across desktop, tablet, and mobile. These wireframes focused on cards for creating natural visual rhythm and breathing room and intuitive browsing, a simple top navigation to avoid overwhelming readers with options and support quick access to the rest of the website's pages, and a clear hierarchy to help navigate the website.

Feedback

Peer Critiques

I shared my prototype with several of my peers and gathered implementable and actionable feedback. The most notable ones were:

"Banner image is too plain and simple"

Peers noted that while the site stands out in color palette, the banner image was too simple and didn't contribute to the site's identity.

"The horizontal drop-down menus are too small and make the site feel cluttered"

Some peers felt that the horizontal drop-down menus made the site feel cluttered and were hard to see.

"The font on the navigation bar could be changed"

Many peers felt that the font used on the navigation bar doesn't match the fonts used by the rest of the site.

"The navigation is comprehensive, but could be reduced"

Peers highlighted that while the navigation is easy to navigate, there are a lot of navigation items, and they take up a lot of space.

"The background color and the tags color could be changed"

Peers noticed that the background color and the tags color were too dark to be noticed, since it was dark blue on black, which made it hard to notice and barely visible.

"The logo size could be increased"

Peers felt that the logo was tiny, and as such was not prominent and not visible as much.

Before/After Critiques

Before

The banner/header image was too simple and plain, which didn't convey anything or add anything to the site.

After

I replaced the old banner image with a new one that I created and drew. I also replaced the banner logo text with something simpler.

Before

The horizontal dropdown menus make the site feel visually cluttered and are too small.

After

I changed the horizontal dropdown menus into vertical dropdown menus.

Before

The font used on the navigation bar doesn't match the rest of the site.

After

I replaced the font on the navigation with the main font used by the rest of the site to make it feel more unified and cohesive.

Before

The navigation bar had too many categories, which could feel intimidating for users and made the site feel visually cluttered.

After

I consolidated categories into fewer primary options, and I discarded most of the secondary options for a more intuitive, cleaner nav.

Before

The background color and the movie tags' color were too dark to be noticed.

After

I changed the navy blue background to apricot and the movie tags' color to tangerine, making the site more visually appealing and visible.

Before

The logo was tiny, and so it wasn't prominent or visible.

After

I increased the size of both the logo and the container, thus making it more prominent.

Handoff

Final Implementation

I successfully implemented and integrated both the TMDB Database and Formspree.io into the system, making the final iteration fully functional and interactive. With TMDB powering dynamic movie data and Formspree.io handling contact submissions, the site now supports real user engagement with film information. The final website includes:

  • A clean homepage with review previews
  • A readable review page
  • A consistent visual identity across pages
  • A layout that adapts to different review lengths
  • A layout that adapts to mobile, tablet, and computer
  • An easy-to-navigate and understand tagging and categorization system

Success Metrics

Because this is a personal site, the metrics are qualitative:

  • I can publish a review in under five minutes
  • Readers describe the site as "personal" or "easy to read"
  • The design encourages me to write more often
  • Visitors can find reviews without confusion
These metrics reflect the goals of a personal, creative movie review website.

Reflection

Key Takeaways:

  • This project taught me that personal projects require UX thinking. Designing for myself - and potential readers - meant understanding my habits, preferences, and creative process and designing a platform that supports them.
  • I learned that great design isn't just about creating something visually appealing - it's about crafting an experience that feels intuitive, inclusive, and genuinely helpful for myself and potential readers who stumble upon my personal site.
  • Iteration played a major role in shaping the final product. Each round of feedback led to meaningful improvements in functionality, navigation, visual hierarchy, and overall usability.

Next Steps

This project laid the groundwork for a more interactive and feature-rich site. The next phase will include:

  • A dark mode, which offers a more cinematic, low‑light viewing experience.
  • Enhanced search functionality to improve discoverability with smarter, more flexible search tools.
  • Refined responsiveness, which further smooths transitions and interactions across all device sizes.
