Wayfinder

✦

Wayfinder is a minimalist utility-first parking mobile app that mainly focuses on reducing cognitive load for users who are likely distracted, tired, or in a rush.

Overview

Large-scale garages create a "visual sameness" that triggers spatial anxiety. Users often forget their level, section, or even which side of the building they entered.

The goal was to design a zero-friction mobile app that requires only two taps: one to save, one to find.

Research

Context Study

Users often forget their parking spot due to rushing or being visually fatigued in confusing environments. Existing solutions like Google Maps, Apple Maps, and third‑party apps are too complex, inaccurate in garages, or fail without a signal, making users frustrated.

I focused on three key areas:

  • Offline capability through on-device sensors to track location without a signal
  • Zero-type interface and a high-contrast UI since users are usually walking while using the app, and parking garages usually have dim light
  • Reducing fatigue, stress, and sensory and cognitive overload that usually comes when trying to remember where the car is parked
The context study revealed that parking is rarely the primary task; it is a high-friction hurdle between the user and their actual destination (a flight, a meeting, or a movie).

Target Audience

I identified three primary users:

  • Travelers at airports
  • Shoppers
  • Concert goers
Each group had different motivations, but all shared a desire for speed, accessibility, and clarity.

Pain Points

Across research, users consistently struggled with:

  • GPS signal loss
  • Tiny buttons and exessive typing
  • Bright light from bright white UIs
These pain points became core design targets.

Competitive Audit

I did a competitive audit on four apps, as these four apps represent the major categories Wayfinder competes with, such as general navigation apps, parking‑focused apps, and single‑purpose car‑locator apps.

Google Maps

Strengths

  • Automatic parked‑car detection
  • Familiar UI
  • Integrates with navigation
  • Works offline

Weaknesses

  • Has buried features
  • GPS-dependant
  • Cluttered UI
  • Not optimized for garages

Key Takeaways

Google Maps has a significant amount of features and is familiar to users, but its vast features and cluttered UI cause too much cognitive load.

Waze

Strengths

  • Driver‑focused
  • Strong alerts

Weaknesses

  • Not optimized for indoor use
  • No parked-car focus
  • Cluttered UI
  • Does not work offline

Key Takeaways

Waze is ideal for driving, but it is not ideal for post-parking tasks.

SpotHero

Strengths

  • Excellent for finding/reserving parking
  • Works offline to a very limited extent

Weaknesses

  • No "find-my-car" feature
  • Marketplace-heavy

Key Takeaways

SpotHero is not truly a competitor for car-location, only for parking acquisition.

Find My Car (This refers to the dozens of single-purpose apps on the App Store/Play Store)

Strengths

  • Simple
  • Single-focus
  • Works offline

Weaknesses

  • Low quality
  • Works best outdoors, not indoors
  • Ad-heavy
  • GPS-only

Key Takeaways

Find My Car is a simple and easy tool, but execution is poor.

Analysis

Project Goals

The mobile app needed to:

  • Reduce friction in everyday parking/vehicle management
  • Prioritize accessibility and ease of use
  • Ensure clear visual clarity
  • Provide clear, at‑a‑glance awareness

Use cases

I want to track my car in an area with no signal

I want to find where my car is in the parking garage among hundreds of similar makes and models

Ideation

User Flow Map

The user flow map focuses on illustrating the step‑by‑step path users undergo when using the app, depending on whether they already saved a spot or are arriving at the garage or parking lot.

Information Architecture

I designed a clear, scalable sitemap to map out the structure and hierarchy of the mobile app, focusing on hierarchical efficiency, zero-tab navigation, and instant utility to reduce stress and cognitive overload. The app is a stripped-down utility-and-task-focused app, and as a result, there are no hidden menus or deep sub-pages present - it is intentionally minimal.

Layout Exploration and Directions

I explored multiple layout concepts and approaches, such as a map-based layout, a photo-based one, and a compass-based one. Through the data from the research, the direction became clear: The app needed to mirror how people navigate parking and vehicle retrieval and how they themselves respond and act towards those tasks. Based on that concept, I decided the compass-based layout was the best choice for the layout of the app.

Why I rejected a few layouts:

  • The map-based layout was rejected because it was too complex to navigate and use; it also added extra stress for users while using it
  • The photo-based layout was rejected because it was unreliable in low light and dim areas. It also did not work offline, for the photos would not load unless they were downloaded
The compass-based layout aligned best with user needs and device capabilities due to its simplicity, intuitiveness, and the fact that it could work offline, which made it less stressful to use - key aspects for users trying to search and retrieve their car.

Design

Low-Level Wireframes

I created low-level wireframes using MockFlow. These wireframes concentrated on large, single-purpose buttons, a compass-style directional indicator, simple icons, and a distance meter using device sensors, for users would likely interact with the app feeling tired, distracted, exhausted, or in a rush. Large, simple buttons and a compass-style direction indicator reduce stress and speed up interactions.

Visual Design Direction

I created several stylesheets to establish the color palette, typography, imagery, and several of the UI elements. The aim was to design an aesthetic with high contrast, heavy weights, and functional colors that worked in a dim parking garage so users wouldn't be blinded by it while reading. The visual design fully utilizes an OLED-friendly black to reduce glare, high contrast accents for visibility, large, thumb-reachable buttons, minimal text, and simple iconography. Typography and spacing were chosen to support quick glances and accessibility.

Handoff

High-Fidelity Wireframes

After some adjustments based on the low-fidelity wireframes, interactive high-fidelity wireframes were created on Figma. The final screens include:

  • A two-tap save/find flow
  • A compass-style direction indicator
  • Offline functionality
  • High-contrast dark mode
  • Haptic feedback for confirmation

  • Interactive, clickable wireframes

Case Study Summary

Wayfinder is a high-utility mobile app designed to eliminate "parking amnesia" by reducing the complex task of vehicle retrieval to a zero-friction, two-tap experience. Developed for high-stress, low-connectivity environments like airport garages and urban decks, the app utilizes an offline-first app that bypasses traditional GPS limitations through on-device sensor tracking. By replacing cluttered map interfaces with high-contrast directional guidance and "one-tap" visual anchors, Wayfinder successfully minimizes cognitive load and spatial anxiety, transforming a frustrating daily hurdle into a seamless, reliable moment of digital relief.

Reflection

Key Takeaways:

  • The most rewarding challenge was the "Find My Car" flow. I learned that in high-stress moments, users don't want a map—they want a compass.
  • I learned that dark mode wasn't just a stylistic choice; it was a safety and accessibility requirement. High contrast and OLED blacks were necessary to prevent screen glare from blinding the user in dim decks, reinforcing the idea that accessibility is the foundation of good UX, not a feature.
  • Offline-first design is essential in real-world environments like parking garages.
  • Reducing cognitive load is more impactful than adding features

Next Steps

This project laid the groundwork for a more functional site. The next phase will include:

  • Creating prototypes and then later a fully functioning website using GitHub and Visual Studio Code
  • Conducting more usability testing
  • Exploring 'Find my bike/locker' modes
  • Implementing audio to guide users better
