Eternal Sigils

✦

Eternal Sigils is a streamlined mobile TCG experience that balances strategic depth with intuitive onboarding, fair progression, and strong community features.

Overview

Many digital trading card games struggle to balance accessibility, strategic depth, and community engagement. Players often face disorganized card decks, unsafe or unreliable trading methods, and limited opportunities for gameplay and community.

The overall goal was to design a mobile TCG experience that supports both competitive mastery and casual enjoyment - while remaining fair, intuitive, and community-driven.

Research

Context Study

Players expect more from modern digital trading card games than just matches - they want a platform that helps them collect, trade, compete, relax, and connect. To understand how well current TCGs meet these expectations, I examined how players interact with existing digital platforms.

I focused on four key areas:

  • Onboarding and tutorial clarity
  • Progression and reward systems
  • Card collection and trading mechanics
  • Community and social features
This study helped reveal where players feel supported and where friction, confusion, or unmet needs still exist.

User Personas

To represent core audience needs, I created two primary personas.

Persona 1: Beatrice West

Beatrice is a competitive, progression-driven player who values rankings, rare cards, and strategic mastery. She wants robust deck-building, fair competition, and a vibrant community.

Persona 2: Mark Sterling

Mark is a nostalgia-driven, casual player looking for relaxing gameplay and short sessions. He wants to introduce TCGs to his family in a way that is approachable and enjoyable.

These personas guided decisions around complexity, pacing, and accessibility.

Pain Points

Across research, players consistently struggled with:

  • Slow or clunky user interfaces lead to user frustration and unsmooth play sessions
  • Unclear or overwhelming tutorials overwhelm and don’t guide both newcomers and returning players
  • Lack of family-friendly or community-driven content lowers deeper engagement and doesn’t encourage shared play
  • Limited language and accessibility support doesn’t make the game and content welcoming to families and diverse players
These pain points became core design targets.

Competitive Audit

I analyzed four major digital card game platforms:

Hearthstone

Strengths

  • Strong brand recognition via Warcraft Universe
  • Polished visuals and animations
  • Large community and player base
  • Frequent expansions and content updates
  • Accessible gameplay for casual players

Weaknesses

  • Heavy reliance on monetization (pay-to-win)
  • Progression system feels grind-heavy for casual players
  • Limited deck-building
  • Visually cluttered menus
  • Aging mechanics with fewer innovations

Key Takeaways

Hearthstone showed the importance of pairing visual polish with simple, intuitive navigation to avoid overwhelming players.

Magic: The Gathering Arena

Strengths

  • Deep strategic complexity and competitive depth
  • Legacy brand with high recognition for decades
  • Integration with physical cards by codes or events
  • High-quality card art and expansive, consistent lore
  • Strong competitive scene

Weaknesses

  • Steep learning curve due to complex rules
  • Resource-heavy client; performance issues on older devices
  • Dense card text and visuals
  • Progression system requires significant time or money
  • Less accessible for casual audiences

Key Takeaways

Magic: The Gathering Arena demonstrated that deep strategy must be supported by gradual onboarding to prevent cognitive overload.

Marvel Snap

Strengths

  • Global Marvel IP with iconic, instantly recognizable characters
  • Fast-paced matches (3 minutes), which are suited for mobile
  • Innovative location-based mechanics
  • Highly accessible onboarding and progression
  • Strong engagement through cosmetics and seasonal content

Weaknesses

  • Limited deck-building
  • Heavy reliance on Marvel IP (may overshadow gameplay innovation)
  • Heavy monetization through cosmetics and seasonal gameplay, and passes
  • Less robust competitive tracking tools

Key Takeaways

Marvel Snap highlighted how short, fast-paced matches and friendly onboarding can make mobile play feel instantly rewarding.

Slay the Spire

Strengths

  • Unique fusion of roguelike and deck-building mechanics
  • High replayability via procedural generation
  • Lightweight install and stable performance across platforms
  • Well-known among indie players and people who love and use strategy-based methods and games
  • Affordable one-time purchase model

Weaknesses

  • Single-player only; lacks community features
  • Minimalist visuals and designs can be polarizing
  • Sparse lore and limited narrative depth
  • Limited accessibility features
  • Replayability heavily relies on procedural generation, which can feel repetitive

Key Takeaways

Slay the Spire revealed that minimalist visuals can succeed when core mechanics are clear, satisfying, and consistently engaging.

Analysis

Market Gaps

From the audit, I examined several opportunities:

  • Inclusive design
  • Engaging narrative campaigns alongside competitive play
  • Fair, transparent monetization models that reward skill and engagement rather than spending
  • Digital play complementing collectible ownership
  • Approachable onboarding combined with meaningful long-term mastery
  • Seamless play across desktop, mobile, and console without any trouble
  • Stronger community features (guilds, trading, co-op modes)

Project Goals

The goal of this project was to design a mobile trading card game experience that is accessible to new and casual players while still supporting strategic mastery, fair progression, and strong community interaction - without relying on pay-to-win mechanics.

Ideation

Oppertunities

Based on research insights, I identified opportunities to:

  • Combine accessibility with strategic depth
  • Deliver seamless play across desktop, mobile, and console without performance issues
  • Build strong community features
  • Offer cards that exist both digitally and physically
  • Add colorblind mode, text-to-speech, simplified UI, and adaptive difficulty.
  • Integrate immersive storytelling alongside competitive play
  • Offer clear, skill-rewarding progression without pay-to-win transactions
  • Introduce rarity tracking and secure trading
  • Build structured tournaments with accessible entry points

User Flow Map

The user flow map focuses on smooth onboarding, clear deck-building paths, intuitive trading interactions, accessible competitive and casual modes, and easy navigation between collection, gameplay, and community.

Case Study Summary

This project focused on designing a mobile trading card game that balances strategic depth with accessibility, addressing common player frustrations such as overwhelming tutorials, unclear progression, and limited community features. Through user research, competitive analysis, and iterative design, I developed a concept that supports both competitive and casual playstyles, offering fair progression, intuitive collection and trading tools, and a community‑driven experience. The resulting structure provides a scalable foundation for future development, including high‑fidelity prototyping, usability testing, and expanded gameplay systems, demonstrating how thoughtful UX design can make a traditionally complex genre welcoming and engaging for a wide range of players.

Reflection

Key Takeaways:

  • This project reinforced the importance of designing for a wide spectrum of player needs without compromising clarity or depth. Balancing competitive complexity with casual accessibility required careful prioritization, iterative refinement, and a strong understanding of user motivations. . The research phase was especially valuable—it revealed gaps in onboarding, progression transparency, and community features that directly shaped the design direction.
  • How essential it is to create systems that feel fair, intuitive, and rewarding, regardless of a player’s experience level.

Next Steps

  • Creating clickable, interactive high-fidelity wireframes
  • Conducting usability testing with both casual and competitive players
  • Refining gameplay flows based on feedback
  • Exploring ethical monetization models
  • Preparing the system for cross‑platform scalability
